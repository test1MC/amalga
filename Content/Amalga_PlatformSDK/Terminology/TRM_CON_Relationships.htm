Relationships

 

This topic describes relationships, which represents associations between two concepts in the Terminology platform application.

In a single terminology, concepts can be related to each other in a variety of ways. For example, the following diagram illustrates a typical acyclic hierarchical relationship structure between clinical concepts in the International Classification of Diseases, Ninth Edition (ICD-9) clinical terminology.

For more information about the ICD-9 clinical terminology, see International Classification of Diseases, Ninth Revision (ICD-9), on the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention site at http://www.cdc.gov/nchs/icd/icd9.htm.

While concepts are important to a terminology, the relationships between concepts provide the real power and flexibility to allow a clinical terminology to consistently and accurately describe a vast range of clinical concepts. Typically, relationships either describe a hierarchical relationship between concepts, as shown in the previous diagram, or describe attributes related to a given concept.

Each relationship contains the following properties:

  • Source concept

    The source concept typically represents the starting or parent endpoint of a relationship, depending on the relationship type.

  • Relationship type

    The relationship type describes the characteristics of the relationship itself. For more information about relationship types, see Relationship types on page 1. Relationship types are specific to a given terminology.

  • Target concept

    The target concept typically represents the ending or child endpoint of a relationship, depending on the relationship type.

  • Terminology

    This property indicates the terminology that contains the relationship. If a relationship is contained by a clinical terminology, it typically describes a relationship between two concepts contained by that clinical terminology. If a relationship is contained by a mapping terminology, it typically describes a relationship between two concepts contained in two different clinical terminologies. For more information about clinical and mapping terminologies, see Terminologies on page 1.

  • Supported language

    Similar to concepts, relationships support multiple languages. One or more relationships, each in a different language, can describe the same relationship between two concepts.
